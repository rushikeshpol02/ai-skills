# L3 Senior PM — Skills Inventory for Review

> **Purpose:** Confirmed L3 PM skill set for JD generation. Consolidated from R&P's current JDs and agency benchmarks (Thoughtworks, EPAM, Publicis Sapient, Slalom, Globant, Accenture Song, Tanzu Labs, CI&T, Amazon, Deloitte, McKinsey Digital).
> **Applies to:** Both Traditional PM and AI Builder PM variants (variant-specific skills noted).

---

> **Status:** Reviewed by Principal PM. Changes applied: 3 skills added, 4 removed, 5 combined, 2 sections restructured. Updated numbering to section-scoped format (C1.1–C9.13) to eliminate cascading renumbers on edits. Added 3 new competencies to C7.
> **Category order:** Strategy → Discovery → Execution → Stakeholders → Technical → Team → Competencies → Domain → Industry. Matches consulting industry convention (Deloitte, Accenture, Thoughtworks, Amazon).
> **Skill order within each category:** Sorted by impact on engagement success (highest impact first).
> **Total skills:** 68 (C1: 6, C2: 7, C3: 8, C4: 6, C5: 3, C6: 4, C7: 12, C8: 9, C9: 13)

### C1. Product Strategy & Vision

> Set direction for the product within the engagement. Define what to build, why it matters, and how it connects to client business outcomes.


| #     | Skill                                                                        | Source                                     | Impact rationale                                           |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| C1.1  | Define product vision, strategy, and roadmap aligned to business outcomes    | R&P L4, Thoughtworks, EPAM, Slalom, Amazon | Without this, the team builds without direction            |
| C1.2  | Outcome framing — reframe client feature requests into business outcomes; answer "what result are you trying to achieve?" before committing to a solution direction; ensure the engagement is oriented around outcomes, not outputs | Amazon (Working Backwards), Thoughtworks, Tanzu Labs, McKinsey Digital | The most expensive mistake in agency work is building the right feature for the wrong reason; outcome framing prevents it |
| C1.3  | Prioritize based on business value, user impact, and technical feasibility   | R&P L3, Globant, CI&T                      | Wrong priorities = wasted sprints and client trust erosion |
| C1.4  | Strategic scoping — determine what's in and out of scope as a strategy decision, not just a change management activity; in agency work, scoping defines what the client gets for their budget and is the PM's most consequential strategic call | Amazon, Thoughtworks, Slalom, agency pattern | Every sprint spent on the wrong thing is a trust withdrawal; strategic scoping prevents it before the backlog even exists |
| C1.5  | Translate business/operational problems into structured product requirements | R&P L3                                     | Bridge between client need and engineering execution       |
| C1.6  | Balance near-term delivery milestones with long-term scalability             | R&P L3                                     | Prevents technical debt and rework downstream              |


### C2. Product Discovery & Validation

> Earn the right to build. Frame the problem as a testable hypothesis, validate with users and data, experiment before committing engineering resources, and ensure design decisions are grounded in evidence.

| #     | Skill | Source | Impact rationale |
|-------|-------|--------|------------------|
| C2.1  | Problem framing and hypothesis definition — articulate the customer problem as a testable hypothesis before jumping to solutions; define what success looks like and what would disprove the assumption | Amazon (Working Backwards), Tanzu Labs, Thoughtworks, Lean Startup | Most failed products skip this step; building without a hypothesis means you can't tell if you succeeded or got lucky |
| C2.2  | Conduct or coordinate user research (interviews, usability testing, analytics, journey mapping) — understand how users behave, what they need, where they struggle, and what they value | Publicis Sapient, Slalom, Globant, EPAM, Tanzu Labs | Keeps the product grounded in real user needs; research debt compounds faster than technical debt |
| C2.3  | Data-informed decision making — validate product decisions with qualitative and quantitative evidence; set success thresholds before experiments, not after | EPAM, Amazon, Deloitte, Publicis Sapient, Globant | Decisions without evidence lead to building the wrong thing; post-hoc rationalization kills learning |
| C2.4  | Experimentation and POC validation — design low-cost experiments (prototypes, POCs, A/B tests, concierge tests) to validate assumptions before committing full engineering resources | Amazon, Tanzu Labs, Thoughtworks, Lean Startup, agency pattern | In agency work the client is paying; every sprint spent on an unvalidated idea is a trust withdrawal |
| C2.5  | Design collaboration in discovery — partner with design to create concepts, test prototypes with users, and validate UX assumptions before they enter the backlog; own the "what" and "why," design owns the "how it looks and feels" | Tanzu Labs, Publicis Sapient, Accenture Song, Globant | Features that skip design validation create rework, user frustration, and sprint waste downstream |
| C2.6  | Competitive and landscape discovery — understand how the market currently solves this problem, what alternatives users have, and where the opportunity gap exists; feed this into prioritization and positioning | EPAM, Amazon, Slalom, Deloitte | Building something the market already solved is the most expensive mistake a PM can make |
| C2.7  | Synthesis and insight communication — distill research, experiments, and data into clear insights that drive product decisions; make evidence accessible to the team and stakeholders, not locked in a report | Amazon, Thoughtworks, Publicis Sapient, agency pattern | Research that doesn't reach the team is wasted research; the PM bridges discovery and delivery |

> **Moved out:** "Own the product lifecycle" (old #6) → moved to C3 as lifecycle ownership is an execution/delivery skill, not a discovery skill.

| C3.8  | Coordinate phased rollouts and release management | R&P L3 | Ensures safe, controlled delivery to production |

### C8. Leverage AI *(applies to Traditional PM variant — all PMs must have these)*

> AI as a productivity tool for PM work. These skills apply to every PM at R&P regardless of whether the product they're managing involves AI. Sorted by impact on engagement success.
> **Sources:** Amazon, Microsoft Copilot, Databricks, Accenture, Deloitte, Thoughtworks, Lenny's Newsletter, ProdPad, Monday.com, HBR (2026)


| #     | Skill                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 | Source                                            | Impact rationale                                                                                                              |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| C8.1  | AI-assisted prototyping — use AI tools (Claude, Cursor, Replit, v0, Bolt) to rapidly create working demos, clickable prototypes, and interactive mockups in hours, not weeks | Lenny's Newsletter, ProdPad, Microsoft, Amazon | Creates the "wow factor" for stakeholder buy-in; 80% of top PMs use AI to prototype before involving engineering |
| C8.2  | AI prototypes as a persuasion and decision-making tool — use AI-generated prototypes to create stakeholder alignment, demonstrate value with real data UX, secure approval, and define the path from prototype to pilot to production | ProdPad, Accenture, Deloitte, industry pattern | The full loop — prototyping without the follow-through is a demo, not a product decision; the skill is influence, not process |
| C8.3  | Prompt engineering fluency — write effective prompts for research, analysis, writing, summarization, and code generation across AI tools | Amazon, Microsoft, Anthropic, all benchmarks | Baseline literacy; PMs who can't prompt effectively lose 2-3x productivity gains |
| C8.4  | AI-assisted research and analysis — use AI tools to synthesize user research, analyze interview transcripts, process survey data, generate competitive analysis, and extract insights from large datasets faster | Monday.com, Accenture, Thoughtworks, HBR | Reduces research synthesis time by ~80%; frees PM time for strategic interpretation |
| C8.5  | AI-powered workflow automation — use AI to draft PRDs, generate status updates, write release notes, summarize meetings, maintain backlog hygiene, and produce stakeholder reports | Accenture, Deloitte, Monday.com, industry pattern | Day-to-day productivity compounding; eliminates hours of repetitive PM admin per week |
| C8.6  | AI tool evaluation and selection — assess which AI tools deliver real productivity ROI vs. hype; select the right mix for the team and engagement | Databricks, Thoughtworks, industry pattern | Wrong tool selection wastes time and budget; right selection compounds productivity |
| C8.7  | AI risk awareness for product decisions — understand when AI outputs need human review, where bias can enter, and how AI limitations affect the product (even when the product itself isn't AI) | Microsoft Responsible AI, Harvard, OECD/NIST | Any product touching AI-generated content needs a PM who understands the risk surface |
| C8.8  | Judgment on when NOT to use AI — recognize when a task requires personal context, empathy, political awareness, or relationship nuance that AI cannot provide; avoid over-automation of high-judgment work | Industry pattern, HBR, Thoughtworks | The maturity signal — PMs who over-automate damage trust and miss context that only humans can bring |
| C8.9  | AI-aware stakeholder communication — frame AI capabilities and limitations for non-technical clients; set realistic expectations about what AI can and can't do; know when to disclose AI-assisted content | Accenture, Deloitte, Thoughtworks | Client trust depends on transparency and calibrated expectations about AI use in the work |


### C9. AI Builder *(applies to AI Builder PM variant only)*

> AI as the product being built. These skills apply to PMs who build AI-powered products hands-on — they code, prototype with AI frameworks, and ship AI features. Sorted by impact on engagement success.
> **Sources:** Amazon AWS, Anthropic, Databricks, Scale AI, Cohere, Weights & Biases, Microsoft Copilot, Hugging Face, industry research


| #     | Skill                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         | Source                                                           | Impact rationale                                                                                                               |
| ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| C9.1  | Functional AI prototyping — build working AI prototypes (agent workflows, RAG systems, prompt chains, multi-modal: voice, vision, document processing) with real model integrations to demonstrate capability, create the wow factor, and test value with users before engineering commitment | Anthropic, Databricks, Scale AI, Lenny's Newsletter              | A working AI demo with real data creates 10x more stakeholder conviction than a slide deck                                     |
| C9.2  | AI prototype to production scaling — define and own the path from AI prototype through stakeholder approval, pilot testing with measurable outcomes, and production scaling (infrastructure sizing, latency optimization, cost management at scale)                                           | Databricks, Amazon AWS, Accenture, enterprise AI roadmap pattern | Most AI pilots fail to scale due to infra misalignment and cost surprises — the PM owns the scaling decisions                  |
| C9.3  | AI evaluation frameworks — define what "good" looks like for AI outputs; build eval systems using implicit feedback (engagement), explicit feedback (ratings), expert annotation, and automated benchmarks                                                                                    | Anthropic, Weights & Biases, Cohere, LangSmith, DeepEval         | You can't ship what you can't measure; without evals you're flying blind on quality                                            |
| C9.4  | AI observability and monitoring — monitor prompts, responses, latency, token usage, costs, and failure modes in production; detect hallucinations, model drift, and performance degradation                                                                                                   | Weights & Biases, Datadog, LangSmith, Confident AI               | Production AI without observability is a ticking time bomb; traditional monitoring doesn't work for AI systems                 |
| C9.5  | AI security, privacy, and data governance — implement data retention/usage/deletion policies, ensure regulatory compliance (GDPR, AI Act), define model access controls, and prevent prompt injection and data leakage                                                                        | Microsoft, Amazon, OECD/NIST, Harvard, Credo AI                  | One security incident destroys client trust; AI systems have novel attack surfaces PMs must understand                         |
| C9.6  | Responsible AI and trust — define fairness metrics, implement bias monitoring, ensure transparency/explainability of AI decisions, establish human-in-the-loop safeguards for high-risk contexts, and build user trust in AI outputs                                                          | Anthropic, Microsoft, Cohere (Safety PM), IBM, Google            | Responsible AI is a product requirement, not a compliance bolt-on; PMs own the ethical boundaries                              |
| C9.7  | AI cost modeling and optimization — forecast token costs, select models based on cost/quality tradeoffs, optimize prompt length and caching strategies, choose when to use cheaper models for lower-stakes tasks, and manage AI spend against client budgets                                  | Databricks, Amazon AWS, Anthropic, agency pattern                | In agency work the client is paying — a PM who can't model and optimize AI costs will blow budgets                             |
| C9.8  | AI vendor and model selection — evaluate and select between AI providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, open-source, client-preferred vendors), assess vendor lock-in risk, design multi-model architectures, and manage API pricing and contracts                                                       | Databricks, Scale AI, Amazon AWS, enterprise pattern             | Wrong vendor choice creates lock-in, compliance risk, and cost overruns; right choice compounds capability                     |
| C9.9  | AI user experience design — manage user expectations around non-deterministic outputs, design for graceful AI failure, implement progressive disclosure of AI confidence, handle latency in real-time AI, and build trust calibration into the interface                                      | Anthropic, Microsoft, Google, Cohere, industry pattern           | AI UX is fundamentally different from deterministic software — users abandon products that feel unpredictable or untrustworthy |
| C9.10 | AI/ML technical fundamentals — working knowledge of model training vs. inference, fine-tuning vs. prompt engineering tradeoffs, RAG architecture, token economics, context window limitations, and agentic workflow patterns; enough to make informed product tradeoff decisions              | Databricks, Scale AI, Microsoft Copilot, Anthropic               | Without understanding the "physics of AI," the PM makes uninformed tradeoff decisions that cost weeks                          |
| C9.11 | Model governance and versioning — track which models power which features, version control prompts and system prompts, define approval workflows before deploying new models, manage retraining triggers                                                                                      | Weights & Biases, Databricks, Amazon, enterprise pattern         | Production AI without model governance leads to unpredictable behavior and compliance risk                                     |
| C9.12 | AI-specific success metrics — define and track technical KPIs (latency, accuracy, cost per query, hallucination rate) alongside business KPIs (user satisfaction, task completion, adoption, retention)                                                                                       | Databricks, Amazon, Weights & Biases, industry pattern           | AI products fail when teams optimize for model accuracy but ignore user experience, or vice versa                              |
| C9.13 | Coding proficiency for AI products — Python, JavaScript, or TypeScript; ability to build and iterate on prototypes, evaluate model outputs programmatically, and work hands-on with AI frameworks                                                                                             | Scale AI, Databricks, R&P L4 JD, Microsoft                       | The builder PM who can code ships faster and earns deeper engineering credibility                                              |
